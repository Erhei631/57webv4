What we learned becoming our own first customer

This year an agent we named Hunter helped us reach a major client we might otherwise have missed. Another agent we named Penny took our monthly invoicing from a few days of work down to less than one, and cut our revenue errors to a fraction of what they had been. Neither of those was the real point.

The real point is that we spent the year running the same AI transformation on ourselves that we now help our clients run. We did it on our own business, on real engagements, with real revenue on the line. We are a technology services firm, and we decided the most honest way to sell this work was to be our own first customer. Here is what worked, what didn't, and what surprised us.

Phase 1: Experimentation

We started with openclaw, an open source agent harness, and built our first SDR agent. SDR work is mostly repetitive groundwork: research the account, tailor the message, find contact info, run sequences across channels. We named the agent Hunter.

The good

Hunter boosted our client research productivity by roughly 10x and delivered real returns. It helped us reach a major client this year that we might otherwise have missed. There was also an unexpected upside. Hunter surfaced client intel that let our AEs reach out armed with the most current and relevant context, sometimes knowing more about the client's situation than the client did.

Then we hit the ceiling. The data we were feeding Hunter wasn't detailed enough. So we had our SDR sit down with different delivery teams to understand each engagement in depth: the value we added, the tech stack we used, what actually moved the needle. Feeding that back into the agent made it meaningfully better.

The bad

openclaw's push notifications into our Slack channels were useful, but they got annoying when they weren't configured carefully. And openclaw itself wasn't the most stable harness, so we started experimenting with others.

The ugly

In the end it is still up to the humans to use the data. If the team isn't motivated, doesn't see the value, or won't embrace it, it does not matter how good the agent is. The other hard truth is that agents are never a one shot deal. They need continuous tuning to keep earning their place. Build and forget does not work.

Phase 2: More agents, starting with Penny

Our next agent handled invoice reconciliation, and it had the biggest operational impact of anything we built. The headline number is that it took my EA's monthly invoicing work from a few days down to less than one day, because Penny sends the invoices and QAs all the related data. But the time savings were not even the most important part. The real impact was a dramatic reduction in revenue error. If we invoice incorrectly, or fail to invoice at all, we lose revenue, and as the number of accounts grows that becomes very easy to do.

This was never just an agent. It was paired with a revenue monitoring dashboard inside our custom ERP. The agent orchestrates across systems: accounting, invoicing, bank transaction monitoring, and our internally built invoice creation. We named it Penny, because we don't want to lose a penny. Almost no one on the team touches Penny directly. It still surfaces exactly the data that management and the AE team most need to watch, and it was one of the biggest process fixes we made all year.

Phase 3: Going full bore on GTM engineering

Armed with these agents and the custom platform we run the business on, we had some early success. That gave me the ambition to go all in on the GTM team and try the GTM engineering everyone in the market is now talking about.

Here is the lesson. The hard part is not the tech. Before you can engineer a GTM engine, the engine itself has to actually work. That meant answering three questions first:

  1. What are we selling? Our offering is evolving as fast as the AI coding agents underneath it, so we had to redefine it.
  2. What is the funnel design, and what is the actual GTM motion?
  3. What underlying SaaS and data platforms do we need to buy or build?

You get the team behind that motion first. Only then do you build the agents to support and automate it. The agents are the last mile, not the strategy.

The real bet: our core business

The biggest threat, and the biggest opportunity, was our core business. In a world where coding agents are minting billion and trillion dollar valuations in a few years, what could we do to differentiate? The answer was simple to say and hard to do. Just go do it.

First we adopted the tools. We let the team experiment with whatever boosted their productivity, and we learned alongside our clients. Then we pushed further and let our internal team build their own tools to solve their own problems. We are a frontier tech company, after all. That is how we ended up building Blueprint, our own requirement capture system, and our own Agentic Builder, our coding agent. Underneath both sits Compass, the proprietary knowledge layer that holds everything we know about how we build.

There were doubts inside and outside the company. Why would this be any different? Why take on the most powerful, best funded companies in the market? Can't Claude Code already do this? Aren't there plenty of tools that do this already? Fair questions, and the natural response.

But as we built and tested on real client engagements and our own internal builds, we saw something. Those tools are products built to serve their ICPs. And as any good product manager or architect knows, product is compromise. You cannot build everything for everyone, so you build for your audience. Some context you expose, and some you hide.

When we built for our own needs instead, we got better results. Blueprint, the Agentic Builder, and Compass let our internal team see all the context and control how that context is constructed. The external tools will never do that, because it is not who they are built for. Think of it as an F1 car where our own engineers take full control, versus an automatic that hides the complexity and just works, most of the time, for its target driver.

This is the whole thesis behind how we operate. We are the ICP of one. We build for exactly one customer, ourselves, and that is precisely why it works. Building something custom tailored to your business gives you a real edge, even against the most powerful companies in the market today. We are not competing with them head on. We are structurally positioned differently.

What's next

Armed with these insights, we are now working with SMB and midsize enterprises to do the same thing for them: arming their teams to stay a step or two ahead of their competition. We were our own first customer. Now we are taking what we learned to yours.
